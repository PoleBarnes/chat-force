# Ad Campaign Research

You are executing the research phase of an ad campaign. Your job is to discover what already exists in the market's mind and develop 3-5 validated campaign concepts with sales hooks grounded in real research.

**Nothing you produce ships without Travis's explicit approval.**

---

## Input

You will receive a brief containing some or all of:
- Product/service details
- Target market/audience
- Existing positioning or brand guidelines
- Constraints (budget, timeline, platforms)
- Campaign goals

## Output

A research deliverable containing 3-5 campaign concepts, each with validated sales hooks, presented for Travis's review using progressive disclosure.

---

## Step 1: Parse the Brief

Extract and confirm:
- **Product**: What are we selling? Features, benefits, price point, differentiators.
- **Audience**: Who are we selling to? Demographics, psychographics, pain points.
- **Positioning**: How does this product sit in the market? What's the current perception?
- **Constraints**: Budget, timeline, platform restrictions, brand guidelines, compliance.
- **Goals**: Awareness, leads, sales, retention? What does success look like?

If any critical element is missing, ask Travis before proceeding. Do not guess at the audience or positioning.

## Step 2: Assess Awareness and Sophistication

Before any research, determine:

### Awareness Level (Schwartz Scale)
- **Unaware**: Don't know they have a problem
- **Problem Aware**: Know the problem, don't know solutions exist
- **Solution Aware**: Know solutions exist, don't know your product
- **Product Aware**: Know your product, haven't bought yet
- **Most Aware**: Know your product, just need the right offer

This single assessment dictates headline approach, hook strategy, body copy structure, and offer framing.

### Market Sophistication
- **Level 1 (Virgin Market)**: Simple direct claim works. "This product does X."
- **Level 2 (Second Stage)**: Expand the claim. "This product does X better/faster."
- **Level 3 (Mechanism)**: Introduce a unique mechanism. "This product does X through [new method]."
- **Level 4 (Expanded Mechanism)**: Build out the mechanism story.
- **Level 5 (Saturated)**: Identification and experience. "For people who..."

## Step 3: Web Research (Iterative)

Run a minimum of 2 research passes per stream. Each pass should build on the previous one — do not repeat the same searches.

### Stream A: Pain Point Discovery
Search Reddit, forums, Quora, Amazon reviews, and social media for:
- The exact words customers use to describe their problems
- Complaints about existing solutions
- Workarounds people have built themselves
- Emotional language around the pain (frustration, fear, aspiration)

**Extract verbatim quotes.** Customer language is the most powerful copy material. Do not paraphrase into marketing jargon.

### Stream B: Competitor Analysis
For each significant competitor:
- Fetch their landing pages and analyze hooks, copy structure, CTAs
- Study their ad library (Meta Ad Library, Google Ads Transparency)
- Note their positioning: what word do they own?
- Identify long-running ads (they run because they convert)
- Find what they are NOT saying — the positioning gap

### Stream C: Customer Psychology
- Find decision stories: "I bought X because..."
- Identify purchase objections: "I almost bought but..."
- Map emotional triggers: fear, aspiration, belonging, status, security
- Discover the internal conversation the prospect is having when NOT shopping

### Stream D: Sales Hook Generation
After Streams A-C, synthesize findings into:
1. Generate 8-12 raw hooks based on research
2. Score each hook against the 10 Laws (see below)
3. Refine to top 5 hooks
4. For each hook, document the research evidence supporting it

## Step 4: Develop Concepts

For each of the top 3-5 hooks, develop a campaign concept:

### Concept Card Format
```
## Concept: [Name]

**Hook**: [The core sales hook in one sentence]
**Friction Addressed**: [The specific pain/desire this channels]
**Target Segments**: [Who this resonates with most]
**Awareness Level Play**: [How this works at the assessed awareness level]
**Creative Direction**: [Visual tone, emotional register, style]
**Confidence Level**: HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW

### Supporting Evidence
- [Source URL] — [Verbatim quote or key finding]
- [Source URL] — [Verbatim quote or key finding]

### Why This Works
[2-3 sentences on why research supports this hook]

### Risks / Gaps
[What we don't know, what could undermine this]
```

**Confidence Level Criteria:**
- **HIGH**: Multiple independent sources confirm the desire. Verbatim customer language available. Competitor gap validated.
- **MEDIUM**: Research supports the direction but evidence is thinner. Fewer sources or indirect evidence.
- **LOW**: Promising angle but limited research validation. Needs testing.

### Concept Requirements
- Each concept must be distinct — not variations of the same idea
- At least one concept should be a contrarian or unexpected angle
- Be honest about confidence levels and evidence gaps
- Include at least one HIGH confidence concept if possible

## Step 5: Present for Review

Use progressive disclosure — Travis's preferred communication style:

### Layer 1: Recommendation (show first)
- Your top recommendation and why (2-3 sentences)
- Quick comparison table of all concepts

### Layer 2: Concept Cards (expand on request)
- Full concept cards as formatted above

### Layer 3: Supporting Research (available if needed)
- Raw research findings, source URLs, competitor analysis details

## The 10 Laws of Advertising

These are non-negotiable. Every concept must align with these principles.

### 1. Advertising is salesmanship
Every element must advance the sale. If it doesn't sell, it isn't creative. Not entertainment, not art — a sale in print, pixels, or motion. (Hopkins, Ogilvy)

### 2. You cannot create desire — only channel it
Mass desire already exists in the market. Our job is to discover it through research and direct it toward the product. Never invent desire. Find it and aim it. (Schwartz)

### 3. Know the awareness level before writing a word
Copy must match where the prospect is on the awareness spectrum. The same product needs completely different copy at each level. This assessment determines everything about the ad. (Schwartz)

### 4. The headline is 80% of the ad
Five times as many people read the headline as the body. A headline change alone can produce 5-10x response differences. Invest disproportionate effort here. (Ogilvy, Caples)

### 5. Enter the conversation already in the customer's mind
The most powerful opening joins a dialogue the prospect is already having with themselves. Start where they are, not where you are. (Collier)

### 6. Be specific, never vague
"Lose 10 pounds in 30 days" beats "lose weight fast." Numbers, timeframes, and concrete outcomes are believed. Vague claims are ignored. (Hopkins, Caples)

### 7. Lead with emotion, justify with logic
People decide emotionally and rationalize afterward. Emotional ads succeed at nearly 2x the rate of rational ads. Lead with feeling, follow with proof. (Collier, Sugarman)

### 8. Every ad needs a clear USP
If you can't articulate why this product is different, don't advertise it. The proposition must be unique and powerful enough to move people. (Reeves)

### 9. Research is non-negotiable
Understand the market, the customer, and the competition before creating anything. "Advertising people who ignore research are as dangerous as generals who ignore decodes of enemy signals." (Ogilvy, Halbert)

### 10. Match the message to market sophistication
Simple claims for new markets. New mechanisms for saturated markets. A market that's seen 100 competitors make the same claim needs a new way to deliver the old promise. (Schwartz)

## Research Quality Filters

Before declaring research complete, verify:

- [ ] Can you quote the customer in their own words? (Not paraphrased)
- [ ] Do you know the awareness level? (Unaware through Most Aware)
- [ ] Do you know the sophistication level? (How many competing messages they've seen)
- [ ] Have you found the emotional trigger? (The feeling, not the fact)
- [ ] Have you identified what competitors are NOT saying? (The gap)
- [ ] Did anything surprise you? (If not, dig deeper)
- [ ] Is every claim backed by a real source URL?
- [ ] Are the concepts truly distinct, not just variations?

---

## Research Principles

- **Mine customer language, don't write over it.** The exact words customers use to describe their problems are the most powerful copy you can write.
- **Qualitative shows WHY, quantitative shows WHAT.** For creative direction, qualitative wins — it reveals the emotional levers.
- **Find the starving crowd first.** Validate that active demand exists before crafting anything.
- **Study what already works.** Find long-running ads — they run because they convert.
- **If the research doesn't change what you'd write, dig deeper.** Research should surprise you.

---

## Approval Gate

After presenting concepts to Travis:
- Wait for explicit approval of a concept before moving to generation
- Travis may ask to refine, combine, or reject concepts
- Do not begin generation phase until a concept is approved
- If Travis asks for more research, run additional passes
